From HGIS to Linked Open Data

Transforming a Historical GIS dataset of Canada's territorial evolution (1667–2001) into a CIDOC-CRM knowledge graph that centers Indigenous territories as enduring places

Overview

This project transforms a set of historical GIS shapefiles—originally published as part of the Historical Atlas of Canada Online Learning Project—into Linked Open Data using the CIDOC-CRM ontology. The result is a knowledge graph that models 334 years of European and settler territorial assertions over Indigenous lands in what is now Canada.

The knowledge graph is a computational model of the colonial process, not a representation of Indigenous relationships to land. Maps and spatial models were historically instruments of dispossession; we use those same tools here to make the history of territorial assertions queryable as structured data. See the Data Model tab for a full discussion of the limits of this representation.

Step 1: Source Data

The source dataset is "Territorial Evolution, 1670–2001" (Nicholson, Whebell, Galois & Stavely, 2020), published on Borealis under CC BY-NC 4.0. It contains 151 shapefiles representing the political boundaries of northern North America across 46 time snapshots. The data was originally developed for the Historical Atlas of Canada.

Step 2: Conversion to Web Formats

The Lambert Conformal Conic shapefiles were reprojected to WGS84 (EPSG:4326), converted to GeoJSON, and compressed to TopoJSON. Each polygon carries properties: territory name, name_fr, sovereign, and year. A companion changes.geojson records 60 historical events with descriptions.

Step 3: Entity Disambiguation

We identified 68 unique territory configurations from the GIS data and grounded them to persistent identifiers:

  • Wikidata QIDs for sovereign powers (11), modern provinces/territories (13), historical territories (20+), and treaties/acts (23)
  • Minted local URIs for entities without Wikidata entries (e.g., the generic "British Colonies" label, disputed areas, the 1927 Labrador boundary decision)

Step 4: GIS Intersection with Indigenous Territories

Using GeoPandas, we computed spatial overlaps between all colonial boundary polygons and 2,057 Indigenous territories from Native Land Digital. This identified 3,216 unique overlap connections between 66 colonial claims and 507 Indigenous territories.

Native Land Digital — Data Sovereignty

Indigenous territory references in this dataset use URIs from Native Land Digital, an Indigenous-led educational platform. We reference territories by URI only—no NLD boundary data is stored or redistributed. All Indigenous territory data remains under Indigenous governance per NLD's Data Sovereignty Treaty. For visualization, use the NLD API directly.

Step 5: CIDOC-CRM Modeling

The final output is an RDF Turtle file using the CIDOC-CRM ontology (v7.3.1) with CRMgeo extensions. The model is event-centric: territorial relationships are mediated through E7 Activities (assertions of jurisdiction), not attached directly to entities. See the Data Model tab for full details on the modeling decisions.

CIDOC-CRM Data Model

The knowledge graph uses an event-centric CIDOC-CRM model with CRMgeo extensions. The core structural pattern is:

E7_Activity [assertion of jurisdiction]
  → P14_carried_out_by → E74_Group [sovereign power]
  → P7_took_place_at → E93_Presence [spatial extent]
  → P4_has_time-span → E52_Time-Span [year range]
  → asserted_jurisdiction_over → E53_Place [Indigenous territory]

E5_Event [treaty or act]
  → P11_had_participant → E74_Group [parties to the treaty]
  → P4_has_time-span → E52_Time-Span [date]

Key Modeling Decisions

Representation, Approximation, and Limits

All humanities scholarship is representation, not inherent truth. This knowledge graph is a computational model of colonial assertions, not a model of Indigenous relationships to land.

Europeans and settlers arrived and began drawing maps—maps that had a profound and sometimes genocidal effect on the Indigenous peoples whose lands they claimed. This project attempts to make that colonial process computationally queryable: to ask questions like "how many Indigenous territories did the Hudson's Bay Company charter claim over?" or "which peoples' lands were affected by the Treaty of Paris?"

To do this, we use the same representational tools—polygons, ontologies, identifiers—that were historically instruments of dispossession. This creates a tension we want to be transparent about:

  • E53 Place is a Western spatial concept. CIDOC-CRM defines E53 Place as "an extent in physical space"—essentially Cartesian, bounded, and static. Many Indigenous relationships to land are relational rather than bounded, include spiritual and ceremonial dimensions that no spatial ontology captures, and are living and dynamic rather than fixed polygons on a map.
  • Native Land Digital's boundaries are themselves approximations created for educational purposes. They do not represent the full complexity of Indigenous territorial relationships, and NLD is clear about this.
  • We classify Indigenous territories as E53 Place as a pragmatic approximation for spatial analysis—it allows us to compute overlaps with colonial claim polygons. This is our modeling choice, not a statement about how any community understands their relationship to their land.
  • The real relationships are richer than any RDF triple can express. Overlapping and shared territories, seasonal movements, kinship networks, treaty relationships between nations, and spiritual connections to land are all dimensions that this model does not and cannot capture.

The purpose of this dataset is to make the colonial process legible as structured data—to support computational historical research into how European powers asserted, transferred, and reorganized jurisdiction over lands that were already home to hundreds of distinct peoples. It is not a model of Indigenous sovereignty, governance, or relationships to land, all of which predate and persist beyond the colonial assertions recorded here.

Indigenous territories as E53 Place (not E93)

Within the limits described above, Indigenous territories are modeled as enduring E53 Places—they are not contingent on European recognition and many have never been legally ceded. The choice of E53 over E93 reflects that these are persistent spatial realities, not time-bound configurations. Colonial claims come and go; the land and its Indigenous relationships endure.

Colonial configurations as E93 Presence

Historical territorial claims (New France, Rupert's Land, Upper Canada, etc.) are E93 Presences—time-bound spatial configurations that came into being through European treaties and legislation, and ended through the same mechanisms. The same physical land accumulates multiple E93 Presences over time.

Event-centric assertions, not direct sovereignty

Rather than directly linking a sovereign to a territory, we route through E7 Activity nodes that represent the act of asserting jurisdiction. This makes explicit:

  • Who asserted (P14_carried_out_by → E74 Group)
  • Where they asserted (P7_took_place_at → E93 Presence)
  • When (P4_has_time-span → E52 Time-Span)
  • Over which Indigenous territories (asserted_jurisdiction_over → E53 Place)
  • What kind of assertion (P2_has_type → E55 Type)

The Royal Proclamation and Indian Territory

The "Indian Territory" (1763–1774) is not modeled as a colonial assertion but as a formal recognition of Indigenous sovereignty. The Royal Proclamation of 1763 was a commitment by the Crown following the Seven Years' War that acknowledged Indigenous land rights west of the Appalachians. It uses the distinct property formally_recognized_sovereignty_of rather than asserted_jurisdiction_over.
